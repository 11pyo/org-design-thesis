Please find enclosed the manuscript "When Does Horizontal Beat Vertical? Information Accessibility as a Boundary Condition for Organizational Structure Effectiveness," which I am submitting for consideration at [Journal Name].

This paper addresses a persistent tension in the organizational design literature: the popular claim that information technology favors flat structures coexists with a mixed empirical record on self-managing organizations, with Zappos' partial holacracy retreat, Oticon's spaghetti-organization collapse, and Morning Star's decades of decentralized success all cited as evidence for opposing conclusions. I argue that this heterogeneity reflects a construct under-specification in the received contingency tradition (Burns & Stalker, 1961; Galbraith, 1974; Lawrence & Lorsch, 1967): "information environment" bundles at least two conceptually distinguishable moderators — task complexity and individual information accessibility — which can and should be separated.

**The contribution is threefold.** First, I formalize the conditional hypothesis that there exists a threshold of individual information accessibility τ\* such that vertical structures outperform horizontal ones below the threshold and the ordering reverses above, with τ\* itself a decreasing function of task complexity K. Second, I develop two agent-based models on Kauffman NK fitness landscapes that decompose the mechanism into decision-aggregation friction and independent-exploration diversity; the comparison between these variants is itself diagnostic of what drives the boundary condition. Third, I test the predictions on a panel of 150 S&P 500 firms (2017–2025) using SEC 10-K filings as the source for organizational-structure, information-accessibility, and task-complexity measures — none of which requires paid databases.

**Relative to prior work, this paper** (a) separates individual information accessibility from firm-level IT investment, which Bloom, Sadun, and Van Reenen (2012) and Brynjolfsson and McElheran (2016) bundle; (b) provides a falsifiable quantification of the boundary condition that Lee and Edmondson (2017) characterize qualitatively; and (c) brings the NK-landscape organizational simulation tradition (Csaszar, 2013; Lazer & Friedman, 2007) into direct triangulation with archival evidence, following the empirically-grounded-simulation template of Davis, Eisenhardt, and Bingham (2007).
